Introduction to New Media


Code and Technology Narrative

My interaction with technology and its effects on my thinking, communication, and creativity were the topics discussed in this project as it explored and demonstrated this relationship to me through text and images using digital design to write and create a narrative that tells about the role of code and media in my everyday life. The outcomes produced taught me that technology is not merely a tool; instead, it is also an agent of change in all aspects of storytelling and identity.

New Media Webtext Experiment

Jacques Derrida's theory of “centralization” and “free play” are what I grappled with most when creating this webtext. I often re-read parts of the text multiple times to grasp how meaning emerges through constructions. I was particularly struck by the notion that meaning shifts depending upon the relationship of one element to all other elements within a structure. This idea prompted me to question how meanings are created, not only in written language, but also in design and digital environments. In addition, the readings by Mark Tribe and Reena Jana provided me with an expanded perspective on the use of technology beyond merely being a communication medium. They refer to technology as a medium for creative expression, which greatly influenced my approach to this project. Instead of treating the webtext as a static form of information, I viewed it as a "living", interactive experience, thereby affecting both the conceptual form of my webtext, as well as the visual aspects of it.

Webtext Design

Plato's Allegory of the Cave inspired this project and explores the relationship between perception, reality, and knowledge through a digital medium. I used HTML and CSS to create a web experience that shows the transition from illusion to understanding as a way of representing philosophical concepts visually and interactively. Along with the exploration of how web design can deliver information, I examined how web design can create an experience. This project challenged my thinking about how a user finds meaning and how digital space can provide a parallel to the process of questioning and finding meaning.

Sound and Image


Sound Composition

In response to 'One Way Street' by Walter Benjamin, I made a sound composition based upon its fragmented and observational character by layering recorded sound against my own words. Most of the pieces were created from the rhythmic sounds of a guitar and an empty plastic gallon being hit; transforming these common materials into exciting entities. These repetitive and percussive patterns create a framework for how these sounds can be manipulated when they are played or interacted with, where the meanings of each sound may vary depending upon where and how they are placed in relation to each other. Other sound elements such as barking dogs and background noise provide opportunities for interruptions and unpredictability, paralleling Benjamin's focus on the texture of everyday life. The composition does not provide a chronological story; rather it offers a number of sonic fragments for the listener to construct meaning from through rhythm, contrast and atmosphere.

The Exploratory Podcast

In this podcast, I set out to answer the question of whether it is better to live in a city or in the country. Initially, I approached the question with confidence as the city was my initial preference based on the opportunities, energy, and activity provided by a city. However, throughout the podcast, I discovered many other aspects that impacted my thinking. Research that focused on the impact of nature on mental health and stress suggested that more tranquil settings may improve mental health and alleviate some stress. Additionally, urban theorists such as Edward Glaeser shared their views on how cities foster innovation and offer vast opportunities for individuals to grow as people. Finally, the third study developed my thoughts further by highlighting the stress response associated with urban living. As I did this, I was not ultimately able to find one answer to my question; however, the process of inquiry led me to question my presumption and rethink the question itself. It became clear to me that the better question concerns individual decisions about which environment suits their needs and goals. Through my research and exposure to the inquiry process, I have developed a greater appreciation for inquiring into multiple perspectives to produce more thoughtful, measured conclusions.

Photo/Text Collage

This project was a photo/text collage and was a response to Roland Barthes’s ideas found in Camera Lucida. I explored concepts such as the operator, spectator, spectrum, how identity is created through posing, and the “adventure” of photography. I paired relevant quotes with images that provided an example of my assertions, by showing that meaning is created during the interaction between the viewer and the subject, not as a fixed quantity related to who created, or took the photograph. The different types of images I used, such as portraiture, action and historical images, were intended to create responses in line with Barthes’s assertions that photographs are capable of having multiple meanings based on the context from which they were viewed while providing emotional and interpretive responses. The use of image and text together was an attempt to create a layered visual experience; I also referenced magazine layouts to develop a design format for my collage. This project has permitted me to better understand how design and visual structural elements may affect the communication and interpretation of theoretical ideas.

Photo Essay

In my photo essay, I used Guy Debord's idea of a dérive as a means of exploring various landscapes without premeditated plans. I went to an art museum and a botanical garden and captured elements that caught my attention, both from structured and natural scenery. I was also able to capture many sunsets, which helped to provide an overarching element of transition and mood in the series itself. While I edited my current composition, I used various techniques in Premiere Pro, such as moving, zooming, and rotating, to create movement and shape within the still images and build an immersive experience for the viewer. The effect of these techniques provided a means to guide the viewer's attention, as well as serve as a means of providing the rhythm of the composition and further reinforce the concept of drifting through various scenes and periods. Through my use of visual editing, accompanied by text and music sounds, the finished product is a free-flowing and exploratory narrative with meaning created by way of movement, contrast, and sequencing instead of being established by a single narrative.

The Vlog!

In this vlog, I conducted an exploratory investigation of the basic but open-ended question of whether vegan brownies would be as satisfying as conventional brownies. Initially, my preconceived idea about the flavor/texture of vegan items vs. conventional left me uncertain regarding my expectations prior to beginning the process. Once I began baking the brownies (with a buddy) and viewed the whole event as a research project, I recorded all the steps and kept a journal of what happened as I prepared my ingredients and completed various tasks along the way. I did not use formally published research to answer my research question; instead, I examined it through an experiential lens, using hands-on experience and a subjective point of view. As we tasted and assessed the brownies we had prepared, my perspective on vegan items changed because I discovered that my preconceptions had been invalidated; thus, I was left with the conclusion that vegan items can provide an enjoyable experience, just as the traditional versions do. This demonstrates that exploration may occur through other means besides formal research and includes the use of experience, reflection, and teamwork. I believe that this Vlog demonstrates how everyday experiences may be transformed into valuable learning opportunities through inquiry and openness to unexpected outcomes.

Essay Film

The essay film I have created looks at the relationship between the ideas of freedom and imprisonment via a set of visual and auditory progressions that take the audience from one emotional state to another. Drawing on theoretical ideas from Gilles Deleuze and Felix Guattari about space and control, I examined how environment can affect a person’s perception of their own autonomy. The film begins with the question “Are you free?” accompanied by natural, panoramic landscapes to show possibility and peace. Later in the film, there are images of imprisonment such as urbanised, densely populated cities; restricted spaces; and social pressures. Each of these examples illustrates how external factors can affect our freedom. The film uses montage and contrasts in order to guide our emotional response to the images; we begin with silence and restraint, and as we progress; we encounter more dynamic sound and images which are uplifting. By moving to images of bright, colourful light and sounds of joy, celebrating connection, and creating community, we see the potential for reclaiming our freedom. Through this project, I demonstrate that the combination of moving images, sound, and editing techniques can be used to convey abstract theoretical concepts through emotional and experiential storytelling.

B-Roll

For this B-roll project, I edited a short video using many pieces of footage taken while on vacation in several locations during Spring Break highlighting the environments and actions that really stood out to me. One of the more striking images was a ruined red JDM vehicle on the side of the road. While its current condition is dilapidated, it represents how something of immense value and importance can be replaced or abandoned due to time and neglect. I also filmed in my hometown’s automotive repair facility. Among the footage taken inside the shop, there was an instance of spontaneity (a squirrel darting through the shop) that brought unexpected life to an otherwise dull place. Conversely, the hardware store footage of a monastery in Texas, including footage of a hermit’s cell or dwelling, gave a feeling of quiet and solitude that provided a different perspective than that which I experience day in and day out. Finally, I filmed footage of a busy Costco Gas Station where I was able to demonstrate the action and routine of the customers and employees that frequented this location. By editing these clips together, I was able to contrast action and stillness, as well as the activity and thought both before and after the act. This project has provided me with a greater understanding of how B-Roll communicates through contrast and composition, motivating me to be more deliberate in the way that I capture, and order my B-Roll in subsequent projects.

Final Project: Why We Sleep

For my final project on sleep, titled "Why We Sleep", I have produced a multimedia work that combines both an exploratory essay-style approach and voiceover narration to explore how and why sleep is experienced in our world of 'digital devices' at all times. Rather than developing a single argument, the work develops through exploratory questions, starting with a central topic and then looking at it from multiple angles through an examination of research-based evidence and personal anecdotal evidence. Informed by Marshall McLuhan, my design of the audiovisual experience creates an environment that simulates both the states of fatigue and alertness of an individual. For example, when the film opens, the viewer is treated to a bright light and the sound of an alarm to suggest the overstimulation of the human brain due to the use of cell phone screens, thus beginning the viewer in a heightened sensory experience. To relate the cycles of rest and wakefulness, I created voice-over readings using AI-generated voices transitioning from soft and calm to energetic. This approach takes its inspiration from philosophers Gilles Deleuze and Félix Guattari, whose theories utilize fragmented and nonlinear structure to parallel the disrupted rhythms of sleep. My use of AI voice generation techniques along with my knowledge and experience in Adobe Premiere Pro, I synthesized text, image, audio elements together to orient the viewer through cycles of arousal, fatigue and recovery. The integration of theory and technical skills along with my exploratory and creative thinking are demonstrated within this project, as well as alignment to course learning goals, including experimentation, revision and work that blends theories/conceptual ideas with 'real' experiences.
